# Critical Raw Materials and Europe’s Energy Transition

## Overview

This project analyses Europe’s strategic dependency on critical raw materials required for the energy transition.

The study focuses on eight strategic raw materials directly linked to batteries, electric vehicles, renewable energy, electricity grids, hydrogen technologies and industrial value chains:

- Battery-grade lithium
- Cobalt
- Battery-grade nickel
- Battery-grade graphite
- Rare earth elements for permanent magnets
- Copper
- Silicon metal
- Platinum-group metals

The objective is to assess which materials create the highest vulnerability for Europe, not only because of geological scarcity, but also because of the concentration of extraction, refining, processing and supply chains.

## Research question

To what extent does Europe’s energy transition create a new strategic dependency on critical raw materials?

## Methodology

The project uses a vulnerability scoring framework based on five criteria:

1. Importance for the energy transition
2. Concentration of extraction
3. Concentration of processing / refining
4. European dependency
5. Difficulty of substitution

Each material is scored from 1 to 5 for each criterion.

The final score is calculated as:

```text
Score = (Energy transition importance × 5)
      + (Extraction concentration × 4)
      + (Processing concentration × 4)
      + (European dependency × 4)
      + (Substitution difficulty × 3)

## Results

The scoring framework highlights two groups of materials:

### Critical vulnerability

Five materials reach a critical vulnerability level:

| Rank | Material | Score | Vulnerability |
|---:|---|---:|---|
| 1 | Rare earth elements for permanent magnets | 100 | Critical |
| 2 | Cobalt | 97 | Critical |
| 3 | Battery-grade lithium | 89 | Critical |
| 4 | Battery-grade graphite | 89 | Critical |
| 5 | Platinum-group metals | 88 | Critical |

These materials combine high importance for the energy transition with strong supply-chain concentration, especially at the processing and refining stages.

### High vulnerability

Three materials are classified as high vulnerability:

| Rank | Material | Score | Vulnerability |
|---:|---|---:|---|
| 6 | Battery-grade nickel | 70 | High |
| 7 | Silicon metal | 69 | High |
| 8 | Copper | 65 | High |

These materials remain essential for the energy transition, but their vulnerability scores are lower because their supply risks are relatively more diversified or less concentrated than those of rare earths, cobalt, lithium, graphite and platinum-group metals.

## Interpretation

The results suggest that Europe’s main vulnerability is not only geological scarcity. It is mainly linked to the industrial control of value chains.

Processing and refining stages are particularly strategic. Several materials used in batteries, permanent magnets, solar technologies and hydrogen-related applications depend on a limited number of supplier countries.

The most exposed materials are:

- Rare earth elements, due to very high concentration in processing and their role in permanent magnets.
- Cobalt, due to concentrated extraction and refining chains.
- Lithium and graphite, because they are central to battery value chains.
- Platinum-group metals, due to concentrated supply and their role in hydrogen and catalytic applications.

Copper remains essential for electrification, but its vulnerability score is lower because its supply chain is relatively more diversified.

## Key findings

- Europe’s energy transition shifts part of the dependency from fossil fuels to critical raw materials.
- The highest vulnerabilities are linked to rare earths, cobalt, lithium, graphite and platinum-group metals.
- Processing and refining stages are often more concentrated than extraction.
- Copper is essential for electrification but appears less vulnerable than battery and magnet-related materials.
- Europe’s strategic challenge is industrial as much as geological.
